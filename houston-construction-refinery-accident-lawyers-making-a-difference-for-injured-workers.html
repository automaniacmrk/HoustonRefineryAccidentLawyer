If industrial dust is in sufficient quantities, it will ignite an explosion when it comes into direct contact with an ignition source. Secondary explosions can occur when additional combustible particles are dispersed into the atmosphere and come in contact with heat. In fact, according to OSHA, most deaths in combustible dust explosions are the result of secondary explosions.

Combustible dust has caused some of the worst industrial disasters in recent history, including a fatal explosion that tragically killed 14 workers at the Imperial Sugar plant in Wentworth, Georgia, in February 2008. Many others were also hurt including those who suffered life-threatening burns.

Trump's administration thwarted efforts to create a mandatory standard for combustible powder in the aftermath of the Imperial Sugar catastrophe. OSHA established the Combustible dust National Emphasis program (NEP) and guidelines for workers to reduce dust hazards. Those voluntary guidelines recommend that employers:

  • Dust must be contained within your equipment.
  • Use a dust collection device to collect any dust that is not contained at the time of release.
  • Clean up any dust not contained or captured.
